> The web treats all non-human traffic the same: block it. That made sense when the only non-human visitors were scrapers and spam bots. It doesn't make sense when an agent is trying to buy someone a flight.

More than half of all web traffic is now automated. [Imperva's 2025 report](https://cpl.thalesgroup.com/about-us/newsroom/2025-imperva-bad-bot-report-ai-internet-traffic) was the first time in a decade of tracking that non-human traffic overtook human traffic: 51%. Most of that is still the usual junk. Scrapers, crawlers, spam. But the mix is changing, and the new arrivals are different.

[McKinsey puts the number at $3 to $5 trillion](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-agentic-commerce-opportunity-how-ai-agents-are-ushering-in-a-new-era-for-consumers-and-merchants) in agent-mediated consumer commerce by 2030. [a16z wrote](https://a16z.com/notes-on-ai-apps-in-2026/) that in 2026, people will start interfacing with the web through their agents. We think they're right.

Here's the thing though. [Anthropic's own research](https://www.anthropic.com/research/AI-fluency-index) admits the sample "likely skews towards early adopters." Their [Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research/anthropic-economic-index-september-2025-report) found that wealthier regions use AI more (1% higher GDP correlates with 1.8% higher usage). The people who are excited about agents are mostly talking to each other.

That gap between awareness and reality is where the opportunity sits. If you start building for agents now, you're not competing with everyone. You're competing with almost nobody.

## Bots are not agents

The internet has had non-human actors for decades. Scrapers, crawlers, spam bots. We built walls: CAPTCHAs, rate limiting, bot detection, robots.txt. If it wasn't human, it was hostile.

Agents are a different thing. The simplest way I can put it: **bots need context fed to them. Agents create their own.**

A bot runs a script. Go to this URL, grab this CSS selector, return the price. Page changes, bot breaks. An agent has a goal. It figures out what to do when it gets there. A [taxonomy paper in Information Fusion](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1566253525006712) puts this more formally, but that's the core of it.

Now, some people will point out that modern scrapers use LLMs too. They adapt to page changes, they're getting smarter. Sure. But a smarter scraper is still a scraper. The distinction isn't about how clever the code is. It's about who it's working for. An agent acts on behalf of a specific person toward a specific goal. A scraper extracts data for whoever deployed it.

This matters enough that [Visa built a whole protocol around it](https://investor.visa.com/news/news-details/2025/Visa-Introduces-Trusted-Agent-Protocol-An-Ecosystem-Led-Framework-for-AI-Commerce/default.aspx): the Trusted Agent Protocol, designed to tell a credentialed agent apart from an anonymous bot. [IEEE Spectrum](https://spectrum.ieee.org/agentic-web) said it well: "The Web today is very much designed for humans... agents don't operate like humans."

So we need three categories, not two. Serve humans. Facilitate agents. Block bots. The internet can't do this yet, and until it can, the agent economy is stuck.

## Why the current internet fails agents

Try booking a flight. You open six tabs, compare prices, cross-reference dates, squint at the fine print. [70% of US travelers](https://yougov.com/en-us/reports/51330-us-travel-stress-report-2025) find booking stressful. [Cart abandonment](https://baymard.com/lists/cart-abandonment-rate) runs around 70%, airlines even worse. Miserable for humans. You'd think an agent could do better.

It can't. And the reasons are structural.

[Amadeus, Sabre, and Travelport control roughly 97%](https://www.businesstravelnews.com/State-of-the-Industry/2025/Part-2-CONTENT-FRAGMENTATION) of global flight bookings. Access is expensive, restricted, built for systems designed decades ago. Travel isn't special here. This is the pattern across commerce. McKinsey put it bluntly: ["If a catalog, policies, and value proposition are not machine-readable, agents, and by extension shoppers, simply will not find you."](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-agentic-commerce-opportunity-how-ai-agents-are-ushering-in-a-new-era-for-consumers-and-merchants)

The workaround everyone reaches for is scraping, and scraping is a dead end. [ScrapeOps](https://scrapeops.io/web-scraping-playbook/web-scraping-market-report-2025/) found 10-15% of crawlers need weekly fixes. [Maintenance eats 60-70% of total cost.](https://scrapegraphai.com/blog/economics-of-web-scraping) You're spending most of your engineering budget just keeping the thing alive. Scraping is a client-side hack for what should be a server-side decision.

The people who own the data should decide how to expose it to agents. Not scrapers poking at DOM structures. The protocols are coming (more on that below), but storefront adoption is where it all stalls.

## OIAT: the four pillars of an agent-ready internet

What does the internet actually need to support agents? We've been thinking about this for a while, and we keep landing on four things: **Observability, Integration, Analytics, and Trust.** We're calling it OIAT. Nobody has a name for this yet, so we're taking a shot.

Before I lay it out, let me address the obvious objection. Agents mostly don't work yet. The demos are great. Real-world multi-step workflows still break constantly. We know. That's the whole point. They break because the infrastructure doesn't exist. The internet wasn't built for them. OIAT is the infrastructure that needs to exist so agents can stop breaking.

[Gartner says](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-08-26-gartner-predicts-40-percent-of-enterprise-apps-will-feature-task-specific-ai-agents-by-2026-up-from-less-than-5-percent-in-2025) 40% of enterprise apps will have task-specific agents by end of 2026, up from under 5% in 2025. The apps are coming. The internet they'll run on isn't ready.

<OIATStack />

**Observability.** You can't improve what you can't measure. Current analytics track humans: page views, clicks, session duration. None of that tells you anything about how agents use your site. Which agents visit? What do they parse? Where do they get stuck? [Quantum Metric](https://www.quantummetric.com/blog/how-to-build-an-ai-agent-ready-website) said it plainly: "Your next website visitor might not be human... brands need the ability to detect and segment AI traffic." [Dark Visitors](https://darkvisitors.com/) is doing early work here. At Toffee, this is where we started, because you can't fix what you can't see. And no, checking whether you show up in ChatGPT results doesn't count.

**Integration.** Protocols for agent-server communication are multiplying. [MCP](https://www.pento.ai/blog/a-year-of-mcp-2025-review) hit 97 million monthly SDK downloads and got [donated to the Linux Foundation](https://www.anthropic.com/news/donating-the-model-context-protocol-and-establishing-of-the-agentic-ai-foundation), backed by Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and Microsoft. Skills, UCP, ACP are all different bets on how agents and services talk to each other. [Visa's Trusted Agent Protocol](https://investor.visa.com/news/news-details/2025/Visa-Introduces-Trusted-Agent-Protocol-An-Ecosystem-Led-Framework-for-AI-Commerce/default.aspx) goes after commerce specifically. Everyone's building plumbing. Almost nobody's actually hooking it up at the storefront.

**Analytics.** Not the same as observability. Observability tells you what happened. Analytics tells you what it means. Agent conversion funnels look nothing like human ones. An agent doesn't browse around; it evaluates and decides. The metrics we need don't exist yet: agent discoverability score, data structure quality, machine-readability index, agent conversion rate. [IEEE Spectrum](https://spectrum.ieee.org/ai-agent-economy) is already writing about an "agent attention economy." The signals that matter are shifting.

**Trust and identity.** How do you know an agent is legitimate? That it's acting on behalf of a real person? [Strata](https://www.strata.io/blog/agentic-identity/new-identity-playbook-ai-agents-not-nhi-8b/) counted over 45 billion non-human identities by end of 2025. That's 12x the global workforce. [ISACA says](https://www.isaca.org/resources/news-and-trends/industry-news/2025/the-looming-authorization-crisis-why-traditional-iam-fails-agentic-ai) traditional identity management "cannot yet distinguish between good bots and bad bots." [Visa](https://investor.visa.com/news/news-details/2025/Visa-Introduces-Trusted-Agent-Protocol-An-Ecosystem-Led-Framework-for-AI-Commerce/default.aspx) and [Skyfire](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20251218520399/en/Skyfire-Demonstrates-Secure-Agentic-Commerce-Purchase-Using-the-KYAPay-Protocol-and-Visa-Intelligent-Commerce) are working on it. But there's a privacy problem too: an agent shopping on your behalf across ten storefronts is leaking your preferences to all of them. Whatever identity layer we build has to handle that.

## GEO is not enough

When people hear "optimize for AI," most of them think of GEO (Generative Engine Optimization). [Aggarwal et al.](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735) coined it at [KDD 2024](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3637528.3671900): structure your content so it shows up in AI-generated answers. Works, too. Up to 40% visibility boost. It's basically SEO for the LLM era.

GEO is real. But it only answers one question: can an agent find you?

It says nothing about whether an agent can understand your product data, complete a purchase, verify itself, or be tracked. GEO is one piece. The companies treating it as the whole strategy are making the same mistake companies made when they thought SEO was the whole digital strategy. You need the full OIAT stack.

## The timeline

<TimelineScrubber />

**Phase 1: Human-only (1990s-2010s).** The web was built for people. Browsers, links, forms, shopping carts. Bots existed as a nuisance. We blocked them.

**Phase 2: Hybrid (2024-2026).** Where we are. Agents are showing up but the internet hasn't adjusted. Traffic already crossed 51% non-human. Agents want to shop, book, research. They keep hitting walls built for a different threat. Companies are asking "how do I show up in ChatGPT?" That's GEO. It's a start. We built Toffee's first product here: agent observability. You can't move to phase 3 if you can't see what's happening in phase 2.

**Phase 3: Dual-mode (2027+).** Two modes: human and agent. Storefronts serve both. MCP, Skills, UCP, ACP, and Visa's TAP are mature. Agent identity works. Analytics are split. [Gartner thinks](https://www.digitalcommerce360.com/2025/11/28/gartner-ai-agents-15-trillion-in-b2b-purchases-by-2028/) 90% of B2B buying will be agent-intermediated by 2028. Over $15 trillion through agent exchanges. The companies that built during the hybrid phase are the ones collecting.

## The window

There's a new actor on the internet. It has intent, it represents a real person, and it's trying to spend real money. Most businesses haven't noticed yet. Some have.

The window is open. It won't stay open.
